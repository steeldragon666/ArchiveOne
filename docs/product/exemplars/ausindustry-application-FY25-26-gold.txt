FIELD 2  DESCRIBE THE CORE R&D ACTIVITY

Portal prompt: "Briefly describe what will be conducted and what is planned to be achieved."

Character count: 2125 / 4,000

The company sought to develop a physics-informed reduced-order digital twin of system frequency response (SFR) dynamics in the Australian National Electricity Market (NEM), coupled to a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) sub-model of sufficient fidelity to support downstream model-predictive optimisation.

At the commencement of the program, no commercially available tool or published academic model simultaneously achieved three requirements. First, solving full electromagnetic transient (EMT) models of interconnected NEM regions at operationally useful speeds (sub-second horizons over 30-minute windows) was infeasible on commodity hardware. Second, existing reduced-order System Frequency Response (SFR) models in the literature had been validated against single-region European or North American networks and had not been tested against the geometry and inertia profile of the NEM, where five interconnected regions (QLD, NSW, VIC, SA, TAS) share frequency via DC and AC interconnectors with step-change behaviour during contingency events. Third, published battery models either treated BESS as an idealised power source (insufficient fidelity for FFR and scarcity analysis) or used full Doyle–Fuller–Newman (DFN) electrochemistry (too slow for MPC integration).

The company proposed to develop a second-order SFR formulation parameterised for NEM inertia, damping and governor characteristics, extended with a region-coupling term that captures cross-border frequency propagation, and coupled to an aggregated-cell equivalent-circuit BESS model with thermal and inverter FFR dynamics. The target performance envelope was: SFR frequency prediction RMSE below 15 mHz on held-out contingency events; cross-region prediction improvement of at least 20 percent over a single-region baseline; and real-time solving speed sufficient to support 5-minute-horizon rolling MPC at 1-second resolution.

New knowledge sought: whether this specific combination of formulation and coupling could satisfy the accuracy envelope in an operational NEM context, and under what inertia and dispatch conditions the model would fail.

FIELD 4  SOURCES INVESTIGATED AND FINDINGS

Portal prompt: "Please explain what sources were investigated and what information was found."

Character count: 2193 / 4,000

A systematic literature review was conducted during Phase 1 (October 2025) by two members of the R&D team, documented in the Literature Gap Analysis (LGA-2025-001). Sources reviewed included: IEEE Xplore (power systems journals and PES GM conferences 2018–2025), ScienceDirect, the Google Scholar index, the USPTO and IP Australia patent databases, AEMO technical publications (2018–2025), CIGRE working group reports, and grey literature from the EirGrid, NGESO and CAISO system operators.

Twenty-plus peer-reviewed references were identified and catalogued. Key findings and gaps: Anderson & Fouad (2002) and Kundur (1994) establish classical SFR theory for single-area systems but not multi-region coupling; Tielens & Van Hertem (2016) treat cross-border coupling for European synchronous areas with a steady-state approximation that discards the sub-second dynamics relevant to FCAS settlement; Egido et al. (2009) provide single-area reduced-order SFR with governor parameterisation but assume homogeneous dispatch and do not handle the heterogeneous NEM inertia profile; AEMO (2020, 2023) quantifies NEM inertia decline but does not publish a coupled multi-region reduced-order model. On BESS modelling, Doyle, Fuller & Newman (1993) and subsequent DFN literature are too slow for MPC integration; He et al. (2011) equivalent-circuit models address speed but lack the temperature coupling required for FFR/droop analysis near thermal limits.

No single published model combined these requirements. Two targeted consultations were held with competent professionals external to the team during Phase 1 and Phase 3 (see project diary entries of 5 Oct and 12 Nov 2025), who confirmed that a bespoke reduced-order formulation would be needed and that the NEM-specific parameterisation and coupling behaviour were open research problems.

The gap analysis produced ten specific knowledge gaps (G1–G10) which were then mapped to the ten technical uncertainties (U1–U10) in the Technical Uncertainty Register. Uncertainties U2 (minimum SFR model complexity), U3 (cross-border coupling coefficients) and U4 (battery model fidelity at MPC-compatible speed) are the anchors for this core activity.

FIELD 5  WHY COULDN’T A COMPETENT PROFESSIONAL KNOW THE OUTCOME?

Portal prompt: "Why couldn't a competent professional have known or determined the outcome in advance?"

Character count: 2320 / 4,000

A competent professional in power systems engineering or energy-market analytics could not determine the outcomes of this activity in advance for three reasons.

First, the minimum model order needed to preserve sub-second frequency dynamics while remaining tractable for real-time optimisation is not a question of applying a known formula. The choice between first-, second- and third-order SFR formulations (with or without governor lag, damping and turbine dynamics) involves a trade-off between bias and variance that depends on the statistical distribution of contingency events in the target system. The NEM's distribution (shaped by its five-region structure, wind and solar penetration, and distinctive frequency-dead-band behaviour) was not available in the literature. The team's first attempt — a first-order SFR formulation taken directly from Anderson & Fouad (Failure F1, 18 October 2025) — produced a frequency RMSE of 47 mHz on held-out contingency events, well outside the usable envelope. A subsequent attempt at third-order formulation (Failure F2, 31 October 2025) overfit the training data with test RMSE degrading to 38 mHz.

Second, the cross-region coupling behaviour of the NEM under contingency events is emergent. It depends on instantaneous dispatch patterns, interconnector loading, and the interaction between DC links (Basslink, Murraylink, Terranora) and AC interconnectors. No published model had quantified these coupling coefficients for the NEM, and a steady-state approximation is insufficient because FCAS markets settle on sub-second frequency trajectories.

Third, the required fidelity of the BESS sub-model was an open question. A full DFN cell-level model (attempted in Failure F3, 3 November 2025) took 47 minutes per simulated hour, making it incompatible with the MPC target of sub-second solve time. But an idealised power-source model lacks the thermal and inverter dynamics needed to represent FFR and droop behaviour near cell temperature limits.

These three uncertainties could only be resolved empirically, by designing candidate formulations, running held-out experiments against historical NEM data, and iterating when acceptance criteria were not met. No amount of literature review or expert consultation would have identified the winning combination in advance.

FIELD 6  HYPOTHESIS

Portal prompt: "What is the hypothesis?"

Character count: 2050 / 4,000

The company formulated three pre-registered hypotheses governing this core activity (recorded in PID-2025-001 at project commencement on 1 October 2025).

H1 — SFR reduced-order model accuracy: A second-order NEM-parameterised SFR model, with governor lag and load-damping terms calibrated to NEM dispatch patterns over the preceding 12 months, will achieve frequency prediction root-mean-square error (RMSE) below 15 mHz on a held-out set of 200 contingency events drawn from the period 1 January 2024 to 30 June 2025 that were not used for parameter calibration. The experiment will be classified as validating the hypothesis when held-out RMSE is less than 15 mHz across both the mainland (QLD–NSW–VIC–SA) and TAS regions.

H2 — Multi-region coupling benefit: Adding an explicit cross-region coupling term that models inter-area frequency propagation across AC and DC interconnectors will improve cross-border frequency prediction by at least 20 percent relative to an otherwise identical single-region baseline, measured as reduction in held-out RMSE for events occurring in one NEM region but requiring FFR response from another. The hypothesis is validated when the coupled model demonstrates at least 20 percent RMSE reduction with statistical significance (Wilcoxon signed-rank p less than 0.05) on a held-out event set of at least 100 cross-region events.

H3 — Low-inertia robustness: The combined SFR + coupling model will maintain frequency prediction RMSE below 20 mHz when NEM total inertia falls below 15 GW·s (a regime increasingly common with rooftop PV penetration and coal retirement). The hypothesis is validated only if held-out RMSE stays below 20 mHz across the full low-inertia band; a conditional result (e.g. holding for 15–18 GW·s but failing below 15 GW·s) is classified as partially validated and the lower bound of operational applicability recorded.

All three hypotheses were pre-registered with falsifiable acceptance criteria and a predefined held-out test set before any experimental work commenced on the activity.

FIELD 7  EXPERIMENT DESCRIPTION

Portal prompt: "What is the experiment and how will it / did it test the hypothesis?"

Character count: 2177 / 4,000

The activity was conducted as a systematic progression of experiments following pre-registered protocols (detailed in Experimental Methodology METH-2026-001).

Phase 3 experiments on SFR modelling (Oct–Nov 2025): four candidate formulations were designed — first-order Anderson–Fouad; second-order with governor lag; second-order with governor lag plus load-damping; third-order with explicit turbine dynamics. Each formulation was parameterised against 12 months of NEM dispatch data via Bayesian optimisation (80–120 trials per model, 5-fold cross-validation) and tested against a held-out set of 200 contingency events dated 1 Jan 2024 to 30 Jun 2025. Performance was measured as frequency RMSE, bootstrap 95% confidence intervals, and per-event residual distribution.

Phase 3 experiments on cross-region coupling (Nov 2025): the winning second-order SFR formulation was extended with three candidate coupling terms — a linear AC-interconnector-loading term; a full admittance-matrix coupling formulation; and a reduced DC-link switching term. Each was tested via a controlled comparison against a single-region baseline on 147 held-out cross-region events, with statistical significance of per-event improvement evaluated via Wilcoxon signed-rank testing.

Phase 4 experiments on BESS modelling (Dec 2025): three candidate BESS formulations were evaluated — aggregated single-cell equivalent circuit with temperature; aggregated multi-cell equivalent circuit; full Doyle–Fuller–Newman cell-level model. Each was coupled to the winning SFR formulation and evaluated on 20 synthetic contingency scenarios designed to stress thermal and FFR behaviour. Simulation wall-clock time, frequency-envelope error, and thermal-envelope error were measured.

Low-inertia regime testing (Dec 2025 – Jan 2026): the composite SFR + coupling + BESS model was tested on contingency events stratified by instantaneous NEM inertia (three bands: above 18 GW·s, 15–18 GW·s, 10–15 GW·s).

All experiments were executed via version-controlled code (git commit a3f7b2c1 and subsequent) with MLflow experiment tracking (internal tracking server) and a test suite providing 80 percent line coverage.

FIELD 8  EVALUATION METHODOLOGY

Portal prompt: "How did you evaluate or plan to evaluate results from your experiment?"

Character count: 1996 / 4,000

Evaluation followed the pre-registered acceptance criteria for each hypothesis.

For H1, per-event frequency RMSE was computed on the held-out 200-event set; a 10,000-sample bootstrap was used to construct a 95% confidence interval around the mean RMSE; the hypothesis was validated when the upper bound of the confidence interval was below 15 mHz on both the mainland and Tasmania regions.

For H2, the RMSE distribution of the coupled model was compared to the single-region baseline on a paired 147-event test set using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test at alpha = 0.05. Validation required a mean RMSE reduction of at least 20 percent together with statistical significance. A failure mode analysis (Shapley-style decomposition per interconnector) was planned in the event that the hypothesis was not validated.

For H3, per-event RMSE was stratified by instantaneous inertia into three bands, and per-band means and 95% confidence intervals were computed. Validation of the composite hypothesis required the upper bound of the confidence interval in every band to be below 20 mHz; if only the upper two bands met the criterion, the hypothesis would be classified as partially validated and the operational lower bound recorded.

Failure-mode handling: each experiment followed a "fail-forward" protocol documented in METH-2026-001. On a failed acceptance test, the team recorded the failure event (F-series log) together with an explicit root-cause analysis, a pivot decision, and a revised sub-hypothesis. Failures F1 (first-order SFR), F2 (third-order overfitting) and F3 (DFN cell-level) are the documented failure events for this core activity.

Knowledge-generation check: for each experiment, the team recorded in the New Knowledge Register what was learned that was not available from prior literature. Three distinct contributions (NK1 SFR NEM parameterisation; NK2 coupled-region term; NK3 aggregated-equivalent-circuit coupling at MPC speed) were catalogued against this core activity.

FIELD 9  CONCLUSIONS

Portal prompt: "If you reached conclusions from your experiments in the selected income period, describe those conclusions."

Character count: 2027 / 4,000

The core activity was conducted over Phases 3 and 4, with the following hypothesis outcomes recorded in the Experiment Results Report (ERR-2026-001) and cross-referenced in the Contemporaneous Project Diary.

H1 was validated. The winning second-order formulation with governor lag and load-damping achieved held-out frequency RMSE of 12.3 mHz (95% CI: 11.4–13.2 mHz) across the 200-event test set, clearing the 15 mHz threshold with statistical margin. First-order (Failure F1, RMSE 47 mHz) and third-order (Failure F2, RMSE 38 mHz due to overfitting) were ruled out. The per-region distribution revealed that Tasmania required a separate governor parameter set due to its hydro-dominated generation profile; this parameter separation was adopted in the final model.

H2 was validated. The full-admittance coupling term improved held-out RMSE by 34 percent relative to the single-region baseline (Wilcoxon p less than 0.001 on 147 events), substantially exceeding the 20 percent threshold. The linear AC-loading coupling term improved RMSE by only 8 percent (not validating); the DC-link switching term improved RMSE by 19 percent but destabilised in low-inertia regimes and was not adopted.

H3 was partially validated. Held-out RMSE was 18.4 mHz (95% CI: 17.1–19.8 mHz) in the 15–18 GW·s inertia band (within the 20 mHz threshold) but 24.7 mHz (95% CI: 22.3–27.1 mHz) in the 10–15 GW·s band (above the threshold). The operational lower bound of the model was accordingly recorded as 15 GW·s, and the failure mode traced to under-representation of emerging DER response in the training data. A sub-hypothesis to address this gap via residual learning was raised and tested in CA-04.

BESS co-modelling (Phase 4): the aggregated-cell equivalent-circuit formulation with temperature coupling was adopted, solving at approximately 2,300× the speed of the DFN model (Failure F3) at acceptable thermal-envelope fidelity. Three items of new knowledge (NK1–NK3) were documented as general contributions to the public knowledge base.

FIELD 2  DESCRIBE THE CORE R&D ACTIVITY

Portal prompt: "Briefly describe what will be conducted and what is planned to be achieved."

Character count: 1818 / 4,000

The company sought to develop a probabilistic prediction model for FCAS (Frequency Control Ancillary Services) market behaviour in the Australian NEM — specifically, the probability that a bid of given price and volume would be enabled (accepted into dispatch) across the six FCAS services, the prevailing market "regime" (flat, volatile, scarcity-driven), and early detection of scarcity events.

Prior commercial FCAS prediction tools use point-price forecasts (a single predicted $/MW price per dispatch interval). During Phase 5 experimentation, these point-price approaches were shown to produce MAPE of 340 percent on the NEM's FCAS prices (Failure F5, 15 December 2025) — the distributions are too heavy-tailed and regime-switching for point prediction to be commercially useful. The technical question was whether a regime-conditioned probabilistic approach could do materially better.

The company proposed to combine a gradient-boosted classifier over engineered physics features (instantaneous inertia, regional dispatch, interconnector loading, DER penetration, ramp-rate stress) predicting enablement probability, with a multiclass regime classifier predicting one of five discrete market regimes (normal, locally volatile, regionally scarce, system-wide scarce, RoCoF-triggered), and a scarcity lead-time predictor providing advance warning of transitions into scarce regimes.

Target performance envelope: enablement-probability classifier AUC at least 0.80 on held-out data; regime classifier macro-F1 at least 0.75; scarcity lead-time detection precision at least 0.75 at 10-minute lead time.

New knowledge sought: whether FCAS enablement and regime transitions are predictable from publicly observable features at commercially useful accuracy, under the NEM's distinctive market-clearing mechanisms.

FIELD 4  SOURCES INVESTIGATED AND FINDINGS

Portal prompt: "Please explain what sources were investigated and what information was found."

Character count: 2126 / 4,000

The literature review (LGA-2025-001) identified a substantial gap between academic electricity-market forecasting and FCAS-specific prediction in the Australian context. Sources reviewed: IEEE Transactions on Power Systems (2015–2025), Energy Economics, Applied Energy, IEEE ISGT conference proceedings, AEMO Ancillary Services reports (2018–2025), AER State of the Energy Market reports, and the patent landscape for electricity market prediction (USPTO, IP Australia).

Key findings. Energy-price forecasting methods (Nowotarski & Weron 2018; Lago et al. 2021) are well developed for energy markets but do not transfer directly to FCAS, which has a different clearing mechanism (co-optimised with energy, six-service structure, contingency-triggered dispatch) and distinct statistical properties (higher kurtosis, longer tails, regime-switching driven by contingency events and inertia). Hierarchical time-series forecasting (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos 2018) is applicable but the hierarchy structure of FCAS has not been published. Regime-switching models (Hamilton 1989; Janczura & Weron 2013) have been applied to energy prices but not to FCAS enablement probability. AEMO ancillary-service reports publish aggregate statistics but no model.

Specific gaps: no published feature engineering for FCAS prediction in the NEM; no published probabilistic enablement model; no published regime taxonomy that maps to operator-interpretable market states; no published scarcity lead-time methodology. Commercial offerings from energy-market data providers use proprietary methods that are not open to external validation.

Expert consultation occurred with two external market analysts during Phase 5 (project diary 8 Dec and 20 Dec 2025). Both confirmed that probabilistic enablement and regime prediction were open problems without an off-the-shelf solution and that the feature engineering, regime taxonomy and lead-time framework would need to be developed empirically.

Uncertainties U5 (predictability of regime transitions vs point prices) and U7 (physical feature sufficiency for FCAS prediction) anchor this core activity.

FIELD 5  WHY COULDN’T A COMPETENT PROFESSIONAL KNOW THE OUTCOME?

Portal prompt: "Why couldn't a competent professional have known or determined the outcome in advance?"

Character count: 2199 / 4,000

A competent professional in electricity-market analytics or applied machine learning could not determine in advance whether the target performance envelope was achievable.

First, whether the NEM's FCAS distributions are predictable at all at commercially useful accuracy was unknown. The team's initial attempt — a direct point-price forecast using XGBoost over standard market features (Failure F5, 15 December 2025) — produced a mean absolute percentage error of 340 percent on held-out dispatch intervals, well outside any commercially usable range. This result confirmed that point-price prediction was infeasible but left open whether a regime-conditioned probabilistic framing could achieve the target.

Second, the correct regime taxonomy was an open question. No published taxonomy categorised FCAS market states in a way that mapped to distinct dispatch behaviour. The team hypothesised a five-class taxonomy (normal, locally volatile, regionally scarce, system-wide scarce, RoCoF-triggered) but had no prior evidence that the classes would be separable in feature space or that their class distribution would support supervised learning.

Third, the feature engineering was open. Whether enablement probability is predictable from instantaneous physical features (inertia, interconnector loading, DER penetration, ramp stress) versus requiring latent state inference was unknown. Initial feature sets based on market data alone produced AUC of 0.61, below the 0.80 target (documented in the Failure log at 17 December 2025). Only after the integration of physics features from the digital twin (inertia, RoCoF, projected frequency envelope) did the AUC reach the target range, and this required iterative sub-hypothesis testing.

Fourth, scarcity lead-time detection was an open problem with a precision–recall trade-off that could not be characterised without empirical testing. Whether a precision of 0.75 at 10-minute lead time was achievable (versus, say, only at 2-minute lead time) depended on the predictive signal-to-noise in the feature set.

None of these questions could be resolved from literature; each required controlled experiments with pre-registered acceptance criteria.

The company sought to generate large volumes of synthetic NEM operating scenarios — tens of thousands of hours of plausible future NEM conditions — that would (a) respect the physical constraints of the power system (feasibility under network flow and frequency-response dynamics) and (b) span the tails of the operating envelope (very-high-DER, very-low-inertia, multi-contingency) where historical data is sparse.

Training a model purely on historical data would leave the downstream components extrapolating at deployment.

The technical question: can a generative model produce scenarios that are simultaneously (i) physically feasible under AC power-flow and frequency-response constraints, (ii) statistically consistent with the joint distribution of NEM operational drivers (demand, DER output, generation availability, interconnector state) in a specified future operating envelope, and (iii) diverse enough to cover the tails of that envelope?

Target performance envelope: at least 90 percent physical feasibility on candidate scenarios under AC power-flow verification; coverage of all 64 cells of a 6-dimensional stratified operating grid; preservation of key empirical correlations (demand-DER coincidence, ramp-stress patterns) to within 10 percent of historical joint distribution.

New knowledge sought: whether the combination of Monte Carlo sampling from a future-envelope prior and a physics-constraint projection step can produce feasible, diverse and statistically reasonable scenarios at operational scale.

FIELD 4  SOURCES INVESTIGATED AND FINDINGS

Portal prompt: "Please explain what sources were investigated and what information was found."

Character count: 2178 / 4,000

Literature review identified three relevant strands. First, Monte Carlo methods for power-systems reliability (Billinton & Allan 1996; Allan & Billinton 2013) generate random scenarios but do not explicitly impose future-envelope priors or enforce joint feasibility on high-dimensional scenarios. Second, scenario reduction and clustering methods for stochastic programming (Growe-Kuska et al. 2003; Heitsch & Römisch 2007) assume a pre-existing scenario set from which a representative subset is selected — they do not address generation. Third, generative models for time-series — Generative Adversarial Networks (Goodfellow et al. 2014; Chen et al. 2018 for wind generation), Variational Autoencoders, and diffusion models — produce diverse samples but do not guarantee physical feasibility and were shown in prior literature to struggle with high-dimensional multi-variable power-system distributions.

Two additional strands were reviewed: physics-informed neural networks (Raissi et al. 2019) which impose physical constraints at training time but are computationally expensive and difficult to scale to the NEM dimensionality; and physics-constrained sampling / projection methods (Li & Li 2022; Grigoriev et al. 2023) which sample a candidate scenario and then project it onto the feasibility manifold.

Published gaps: no published method combined a future-envelope Monte Carlo prior with a NEM-specific physics-projection step; no published evaluation of feasibility rates on NEM-scale systems; no published diversity benchmark for NEM synthetic scenarios.

Failure F6 (22 December 2025) documents the team's first attempt: unconstrained Monte Carlo sampling of NEM scenarios produced only 0.5 percent feasibility when tested against AC power flow (i.e. 99.5 percent of candidate scenarios violated operational constraints). This confirmed the need for an explicit physics-projection step.

Expert consultation during Phase 6 (project diary 23 Dec 2025) with an external power-systems simulation specialist confirmed that physics-constrained scenario generation at NEM scale was an open problem without a published solution.

Uncertainty U6 anchors this core activity.

FIELD 5  WHY COULDN’T A COMPETENT PROFESSIONAL KNOW THE OUTCOME?

Portal prompt: "Why couldn't a competent professional have known or determined the outcome in advance?"

Character count: 2035 / 4,000

A competent professional in operations research, power-systems simulation or statistical machine learning could not determine in advance whether 90 percent physical feasibility was achievable, or at what computational cost.

The space of NEM operating scenarios at sub-hourly resolution is very high-dimensional: five regions × multiple fuel-type generation × DER penetration × demand × interconnector state × contingency pattern. Naive Monte Carlo in this space produces feasibility rates that vanish with dimensionality — Failure F6 confirmed a feasibility rate below 1 percent for the NEM.

Physics-constrained approaches offer higher feasibility rates but at a computational cost that depends on (a) the feasibility-projection geometry (convex, non-convex, disconnected) and (b) the dimensionality of the active-constraint set. Neither was characterised in the literature for the NEM. Whether a projection step would preserve the statistical properties of the prior distribution (or bias the scenario set toward the feasibility boundary, creating "corner-case-only" scenarios) was unknown.

Diversity was a related open question. A high feasibility rate is trivially achievable by sampling only scenarios close to historical operation, but this would fail to cover the tails of the future operating envelope. The trade-off between feasibility, diversity and statistical consistency with the prior was empirically unknown for the NEM.

Nor was it known whether a single projection step would suffice or whether iterative projection (with risk of convergence failure) would be required. The answer depends on the NEM's specific network topology and constraint structure and could only be determined by experiment.

Finally, computational budget mattered operationally. Generating tens of thousands of scenarios overnight on commodity hardware imposes a wall-clock budget per scenario that constrains the acceptable projection complexity. Whether the target feasibility rate could be achieved within that budget was itself an unknown.

A competent professional in operations research, stochastic optimisation or power-systems economics could not determine in advance whether the target performance envelope was achievable.

First, whether the MPC formulation converges to operationally executable bids at the required speed (sub-second per dispatch interval, for bid submission deadlines of 65 seconds before each 5-minute dispatch) depends on the convexity structure of the co-optimisation over the scenario tree. Third, whether the co-optimisation improves over sequential bidding depends on the structure of the FCAS-energy interaction. In regimes where FCAS is abundantly enabled at low prices, sequential bidding is near-optimal; in scarcity regimes, co-optimisation is materially better. The distribution of these regimes in real NEM operation was not published and had to be characterised empirically.
